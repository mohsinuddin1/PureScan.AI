  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why do cooling ingredients like Menthol, Camphor, and Phenol cause chronic lip peeling?</h3>
  <p>One of the most searched everyday questions across Europe and the United States is why medicated or cooling lip balms cause a vicious cycle of dryness. Ingredients like Menthol, Camphor, Eucalyptus, and Phenol are added to provide a refreshing tingling sensation that feels like active healing. In reality, dermatological studies show these volatile phenols act as mild exfoliants and neuro-irritants on the lip vermilion border—a delicate zone lacking sebaceous glands and protective keratin. Repeated application damages the thin stratum corneum, causing micro-cracks, inflammation, and irritant contact cheilitis that forces you to constantly reapply.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do mineral oils and petrolatum create lip balm dependency without repairing the barrier?</h3>
  <p>Many commercial lip sticks rely primarily on refined mineral oil, paraffin, or pure petrolatum. While inert and effective at sealing moisture in, these hydrocarbon occlusives do not actively nourish or rebuild lip cells. When applied repeatedly over dehydrated lips without essential lipids (like ceramides, jojoba, or squalane), an impenetrable barrier prevents atmospheric moisture absorption while down-regulating natural epidermal lipid synthesis. Over time, your lips become physiologically reliant on the external film, feeling raw and intensely tight the moment the balm wears off.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do salty ultra-processed snacks and dietary acids dehydrate and irritate your lips?</h3>
  <p>Lip health isn't dictated solely by topical cosmetics; daily snacks and drinks play a direct role. Ultra-processed foods seasoned with heavy sodium chloride, artificial flavorings, and titratable acids (such as citric acid in sour candies or sodas) come into direct contact with lip mucosa. High surface salinity pulls intracellular water out of delicate lip cells through osmotic dehydration. Combined with inflammatory food colorants (like Red 40 or Yellow 5), frequent snack exposure can trigger perioral dermatitis and angular cheilitis around lip corners.</p>
