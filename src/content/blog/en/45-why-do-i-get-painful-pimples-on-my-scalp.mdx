  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why is dry shampoo causing my scalp breakouts?</h3>
  <p>Many people rely on dry shampoo to extend the time between washes, but they overlook how it affects the scalp's delicate ecosystem. Aerosol dry shampoos use starches (like rice or corn starch) and propellants (butane, isobutane) to absorb excess oil. However, they do not actually clean the hair. Instead, they form a paste-like residue that traps sebum, sweat, and dead skin cells directly against the scalp. If left unwashed for days, this occlusive layer clogs the large hair follicles on your head, creating a low-oxygen environment perfect for *Cutibacterium acnes* bacteria and *Malassezia* yeast to multiply, triggering deep, painful cysts.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do styling products cause "pomade acne" along the hairline?</h3>
  <p>The physical migration of styling products plays a massive role in hairline acne. "Pomade acne" is a specific type of breakout caused by heavy, comedogenic hair products. Edge controls, waxes, gels, and pomades are often formulated with thick occlusive ingredients like petrolatum, beeswax, and isopropyl myristate. Throughout the day, as you sweat or touch your hair, these heavy waxes melt and migrate down your forehead, temples, and neck. They enter the pores along the hairline, forming a waterproof seal that blocks the follicular opening and forces sebum to back up, resulting in persistent closed comedones (whiteheads) and inflamed pustules.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do sweaty workouts and sugary diets trigger scalp cysts?</h3>
  <p>If your hair products are non-comedogenic but you still get painful scalp pimples, the culprit might be your post-workout habits and diet. Leaving sweat to dry in your hair or wearing tight, sweaty baseball caps and helmets creates a warm, moist microclimate that breeds bacteria. Internally, consuming high-glycemic carbohydrates (like sugary snacks) and dairy causes rapid spikes in Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1). High levels of IGF-1 stimulate the sebaceous glands on your scalp to grow (hyperplasia) and pump out excessive amounts of thick, inflammatory sebum. This internal hormonal cascade often manifests as severe, tender nodules hidden under your hair.</p>
