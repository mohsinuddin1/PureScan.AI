  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">What are the health risks of synthetic food dyes in children?</h3>
  <p>The controversy surrounding synthetic food dyes stems from decades of clinical research linking petroleum-derived artificial colors to neurobehavioral issues. The renowned Southampton Study demonstrated a direct correlation between the consumption of artificial food colors—specifically Red 40, Yellow 5, and Yellow 6—and increased hyperactivity, impulsivity, and shortened attention spans in young children. In addition to behavioral changes, some children experience hypersensitivity reactions, including skin hives (urticaria) and exacerbation of asthma symptoms, particularly in response to Tartrazine (Yellow 5).</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why is the FDA banning Red 40 and other food dyes in 2026?</h3>
  <p>The push for an FDA food dye ban in 2026 has been catalyzed by growing public pressure and state-level legislative action, such as California's recent move to ban FD&C Red No. 3 in foods due to its association with animal carcinogenicity. While a total federal ban on Red 40 is not yet fully enacted, regulatory bodies are pushing for a voluntary phase-out by food manufacturers over the next few years. In stark contrast to the US, the European Union has long enforced a precautionary approach, requiring explicit warning labels on foods with synthetic dyes, which has successfully pressured European manufacturers to quietly switch to safer natural alternatives like beetroot, annatto, and paprika extracts.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Red 40 vs Natural Food Coloring: How can you spot hidden dyes?</h3>
  <p>Identifying hidden synthetic dyes on nutrition labels can be challenging because manufacturers often disguise them using alternative chemical names (such as Allura Red AC for Red 40 or Sunset Yellow FCF for Yellow 6). These artificial dyes are not just in brightly colored candies; they are frequently hidden in unexpected savory foods like pickles, salad dressings, cheese crackers, and even children's medicine to artificially enhance visual appeal. Transitioning to natural food colorings derived from plant sources—such as anthocyanins from beetroot, carotenoids from annatto, and curcumin from turmeric—offers a vibrant, completely safe alternative without the neurological and immunological risks.</p>
