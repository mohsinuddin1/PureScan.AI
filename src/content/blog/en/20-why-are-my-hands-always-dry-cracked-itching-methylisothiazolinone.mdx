  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why is Methylisothiazolinone (MI) one of Europe's top triggers for hand eczema and dermatitis?</h3>
  <p>Across Europe, one of the most frequently searched skin problems is unexplained hand eczema and peeling fingertips. A primary culprit is Methylisothiazolinone (MI or MIT)—a broad-spectrum biocide widely used in liquid hand soaps, sanitizing gels, and washing liquids. European dermatological registries identify MI as a leading contact allergen. When applied repeatedly to hands, even low ppm concentrations sensitize immune T-cells in the stratum corneum, triggering itchy, red, blistering dermatitis and painful cracks that persist long after washing.</p>
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do alkaline surfactants in antibacterial hand soaps destroy natural skin lipids?</h3>
  <p>Many commercial liquid hand cleansers use harsh anionic surfactants like Sodium Lauryl Sulfate (SLS) combined with alkaline builders that raise pH above 8.5. Healthy hand skin maintains an acidic pH of 4.5 to 5.5, which keeps protective enzymes active and intercellular ceramides intact. Frequent washing with high-pH degreasing soaps dissolves epidermal fatty acids and denatures structural keratin. Without protective intercellular cement, moisture rapidly evaporates from the dorsum of the hands, leaving them rough, fissured, and vulnerable to external irritants.</p>
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do dietary sulfites, canned foods, and processed snacks trigger hand flares from within?</h3>
  <p>Hand skin condition is also modulated by internal gastrointestinal and immune responses. For individuals sensitized to contact irritants, consuming ultra-processed foods containing high sulfite preservatives or trace nickel (frequently leached from canned foods and refined chocolates) can precipitate systemic contact dermatitis. This internal immune activation often presents as dyshidrotic eczema—intensely itchy, fluid-filled vesicles on the sides of the fingers and palms that crack and peel as they heal.</p>
