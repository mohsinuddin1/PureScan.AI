  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why do scented face creams and perfumes cause phototoxic dark patches (Berloque dermatitis) under sunny skies?</h3>
  <p>Across Greece, the Mediterranean, and sunny coastal regions, millions search for solutions to persistent facial sunspots and upper lip darkening ("panades"). Yet many inadvertently worsen pigmentation by wearing scented day creams, botanical mists, or perfumes before heading outdoors. Fragrant plant extracts containing furocoumarins—especially bergamot oil (*Citrus aurantium bergamia*), limonene, and angelica root—are potent photosensitizers. When exposed to UVA sunlight, they absorb light energy and transfer toxic singlet oxygen directly to basal epidermal cells, causing acute cell injury followed by deep, brownish-gray melanocytic staining.</p>
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why do aggressive summer chemical peels and unbuffered glycolic acids trigger rebound hyperpigmentation?</h3>
  <p>In an urgent effort to clear summer dark spots, many turn to high-strength unbuffered alpha-hydroxy acids (AHA) or harsh skin lighteners during high-UV months. While chemical exfoliation accelerates epidermal turnover, unbuffered low-pH peels thin the stratum corneum's natural photoprotective shield. When freshly exfoliated, vulnerable skin meets intense summer radiation, underlying melanocytes interpret the UV penetration as tissue trauma and flood the epidermis with excess melanin—a phenomenon known as post-inflammatory rebound hyperpigmentation.</p>
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do dietary glycemic spikes and low polyphenol intake weaken the skin's resistance to UV oxidative stress?</h3>
  <p>Ultraviolet radiation continuously generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) within dermal capillaries and keratinocytes. To neutralize this oxidative bombardment, skin cells require endogenous antioxidants and dietary polyphenols. Ultra-processed diets loaded with refined carbohydrates and pro-inflammatory seed oils create advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) that degrade collagen and sensitize melanocytes. Conversely, diets abundant in Mediterranean polyphenols—such as extra virgin olive oil, pomegranate, and green tea catechins—bolster systemic antioxidant reserves to help curb UV-triggered pigment darkening.</p>
