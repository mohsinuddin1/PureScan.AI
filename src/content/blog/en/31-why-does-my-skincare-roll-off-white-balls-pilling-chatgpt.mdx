  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">What chemical ingredients cause skincare to pill and form rubbery white balls?</h3>
  <p>Skincare pilling is primarily triggered by high-molecular-weight film-formers and occlusive polymers designed to create a smooth surface finish. Ingredients such as Carbomer, Acrylates/C10-30 Alkyl Acrylate Crosspolymer, Xanthan Gum, and heavy silicones like Dimethicone form an unabsorbed mesh over the stratum corneum. When multiple film-forming products are layered or rubbed prematurely, mechanical friction shatters this delicate surface film into visible white particles.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why does mixing acidic Vitamin C serums with gel moisturizers trigger instant pilling?</h3>
  <p>Synthetic thickening polymers like Carbomer rely on a neutralized, slightly alkaline pH (6.0 to 7.0) to maintain their expanded gel structure. When layered directly over a highly acidic serum such as L-Ascorbic Acid (pH 2.5 to 3.0) or high-strength exfoliation acids, the sudden drop in pH collapses the polymer matrix. This structural breakdown causes the gel thickeners to precipitate out of solution and roll off the skin.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How does underlying skin dehydration and omega-3 deficiency worsen product pilling?</h3>
  <p>Dehydrated, keratin-stiffened skin lacking essential dietary omega-3 fatty acids creates a rough, uneven stratum corneum with high surface friction. Without adequate cellular hydration and barrier lipids, emollients fail to penetrate uniformly, leaving active polymers pooled on the surface where they quickly clump together.</p>
