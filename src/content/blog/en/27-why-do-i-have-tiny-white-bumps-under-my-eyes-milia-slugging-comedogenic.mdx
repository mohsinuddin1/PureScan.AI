    <p className="text-slate-700 font-medium">Those tiny, pearl-like white or yellowish bumps beneath your eyes and across your upper cheeks are **milia** (subepidermal keratin cysts)—not acne whiteheads, pimples, or clogged pores. Unlike regular blemishes, milia lack an exit duct, meaning that squeezing or popping them only damages delicate periorbital blood vessels and causes scarring without releasing the trapped cyst. They develop when dead keratinized skin cells fail to shed properly and become entombed beneath the stratum corneum. The leading culprits are heavy occlusive "slugging" ointments (pure petrolatum, paraffin wax, mineral oils) and rich, highly comedogenic eye creams containing pore-clogging esters like isopropyl myristate, acetylated lanolin, and ethylhexyl palmitate.   <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why do heavy eye creams and occlusive 'slugging' ointments trap keratin under delicate periorbital skin?</h3>
  <p>The skin around the eyes is the thinnest on the human body—measuring roughly 0.5 millimeters—and possesses very few sebaceous glands. Because it easily feels dry, many people layer rich, buttery eye creams or follow social media trends by "slugging" with thick petroleum jelly or occlusive balms. While occlusives prevent transepidermal water loss on rugged facial areas, applying heavy hydrocarbons and waxes over thin periorbital tissue creates an impermeable seal. This smothering film halts normal corneocyte detachment, forcing shedding keratin flakes into the infundibulum of fine vellus hair follicles and eccrine sweat ducts until they solidify into hard keratin pearls.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why can't you pop or squeeze milia like regular whiteheads or pimples?</h3>
  <p>A conventional acne whitehead (closed comedo) is an accumulation of liquid sebum and bacteria inside an open follicle lumen, which can be extracted under pressure. A milium, by contrast, is a miniature cyst consisting of hardened keratin scales enclosed inside a tough epithelial capsule entirely beneath intact surface skin. Because there is no pore opening connecting the cyst to the surface, applying mechanical pressure squeezes the surrounding tissue instead of the milium. Squeezing ruptures fragile periorbital capillaries, resulting in lingering bruising, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, and permanent micro-scarring.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do dairy hormones (IGF-1) and low omega-3 levels accelerate epidermal keratin clumping?</h3>
  <p>Epidermal turnover relies on a balanced hormonal environment and fluid cell membranes. Consuming large quantities of conventional dairy products—particularly whey protein and skim milk—spikes Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1). High IGF-1 signaling stimulates keratinocyte hyperproliferation and increases epidermal cellular cohesion, making dead cells stick together rather than sloughing off smoothly. Simultaneously, a dietary deficiency in anti-inflammatory omega-3 fatty acids reduces cell membrane fluidity, further promoting keratin retention.</p>
