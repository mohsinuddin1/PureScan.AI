  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why do chemical UV filters like Avobenzone and Octocrylene migrate and sting delicate eye tissues?</h3>
  <p>One of the most frequent complaints across Europe and the United States is sunscreen burning the eyes hours after application. Chemical (organic) UV filters such as Avobenzone, Octocrylene, and Homosalate are oil-soluble compounds designed to absorb ultraviolet radiation. Throughout the day, normal facial sebum, sweat, and blinking cause these mobile molecules to creep across the eyelid and dissolve into the tear film. Once in contact with the sensitive conjunctiva and cornea, they activate transient receptor potential (TRP) nerve channels, causing burning, watery eyes, and blurred vision. Mineral filters like non-nano Zinc Oxide remain inert on the skin surface without stinging.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Why do many sunscreens clog pores and trigger acne or subclinical bumps?</h3>
  <p>Achieving a high SPF rating without a chalky white cast requires manufacturers to disperse UV filters in heavy synthetic esters and occlusive vehicles. Ingredients such as Isopropyl Myristate, Ethylhexyl Palmitate, and Isopropyl Palmitate are frequently added to chemical and hybrid sunscreens. While they create a silky initial feel, dermatological comedogenicity studies show these fatty acid esters rapidly fuse with dead corneocytes inside pore canals, forming microcomedones. Trapped beneath an occlusive SPF film, sebum and sweat foster Cutibacterium acnes overgrowth, leading to stubborn closed comedones and painful inflammatory breakouts.</p>
  
  <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">How do dietary Advanced Glycation End-products (AGEs) and refined sugars accelerate sun damage from the inside out?</h3>
  <p>Topical SPF only protects against external photons; structural skin resilience depends heavily on internal biochemistry. When you consume diets high in ultra-processed sugars, high-fructose syrups, and fried foods rich in dietary Advanced Glycation End-products (AGEs), glucose molecules permanently bind to dermal collagen and elastin proteins. This process—known as glycation—stiffens collagen fibers and impairs the skin's natural antioxidant defenses. Glycated skin loses its elasticity and becomes far more susceptible to UV-induced oxidative stress, leading to premature wrinkles, sagging, and stubborn sunspots even when daily sunscreen is applied.</p>
